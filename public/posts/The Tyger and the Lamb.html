The Tyger & The Lamb

by William Blake

A study of innocence and experience, the gentle and the terrifying.

The Poet: William Blake (1757-1827)

William Blake was an English poet, painter, and printmaker. Largely unrecognised during his lifetime, Blake is now considered a seminal figure in the history of the poetry and visual arts of the Romantic Age. He lived in London his entire life (except for three years in Felpham) and held strong, often radical views on religion, society, and politics.

Core Preoccupations: Blake was deeply spiritual but hostile to organised religion. He explored the "two contrary states of the human soul" through his collections Songs of Innocence (1789) and Songs of Experience (1794), where "The Lamb" and "The Tyger" appear, respectively. He valued imagination above reason and believed in a personal, mystical connection to the divine.

The Literary Period: Romanticism (c. 1785‚Äì1832)

Blake was a key early figure in the Romantic movement, which was a reaction against the Enlightenment's focus on logic and reason. Romantics championed emotion, individualism, and the beauty and power of the natural world.

Main Features of Romanticism found in these poems:

  • Emotion over Reason: The poems are driven by feelings of wonder, tenderness ("The Lamb"), and awe-filled terror ("The Tyger").
  • The Sublime: "The Tyger" perfectly captures the sublime ‚Äì an experience of nature that is both terrifying and beautiful, overwhelming the mind.
  • Imagination: Blake uses powerful, imaginative visions (a tiger "burning bright," a divine blacksmith) to explore profound questions.
  • Spirituality & Mysticism: Both poems grapple with the nature of God and creation, not through doctrine, but through personal, mystical questioning.

The Tyger

Title Explanation: "The Tyger"

"Tyger": The archaic spelling (older form of "Tiger") immediately sets a mythical, timeless, and slightly exotic tone. It distances the creature from a simple zoo animal and elevates it to a symbolic level.

Connotations: The word itself evokes power, ferocity, danger, and a wild, untamable beauty. It is the embodiment of raw, primal energy.

Interpretive Angles:

  1. Literal: At its simplest, the poem is about a tiger, wondering about the powerful being that could create such a fearsome animal.
  2. Symbolic: The "Tyger" represents the fierce, terrifying, and dangerous aspects of the world and human nature‚Äîwhat Blake called "Experience." It symbolizes evil, violence, artistic creation, revolutionary energy, and the parts of God's creation that are not gentle or kind.

Poetic Form & Prosody

  • Form: Lyric poem, consisting of six quatrains (four-line stanzas).
  • Rhyme Scheme: AABB CCDD EEFF etc. (rhyming couplets). This simple, almost nursery-rhyme-like scheme creates a stark contrast with the poem's complex and terrifying subject matter.
  • Meter: Primarily Trochaic Tetrameter Catalectic. This means each line has roughly four "trochees" (a stressed syllable followed by an unstressed one: DA-dum), but the final unstressed syllable is dropped (catalectic).
    • Example: Ty-ger | Ty-ger | burn-ing | bright
    This creates a forceful, hammering, chant-like rhythm, mimicking a blacksmith's hammer or a beating heart.
  • Structural Features: The first and last stanzas are nearly identical, creating a powerful frame. The change from "Could frame" to "Dare frame" in the final stanza deepens the poem's central question from one of ability to one of audacity and courage.
Quick Tip: How to Read Meter

Meter is the rhythm of a poem. A "foot" is a basic unit of rhythm (like a trochee: STRESSED-unstressed). "Tetrameter" means there are four feet per line. To find the meter, read the line aloud and listen for the natural beats or stresses. Tapping it out can help!

The Lamb

Title Explanation: "The Lamb"

"Lamb": The lamb is a powerful and traditional symbol in Western and Christian culture.

Connotations: It evokes purity, gentleness, innocence, vulnerability, and sacrifice. It is the complete opposite of the tiger's ferocity.

Interpretive Angles:

  1. Literal: The poem is addressed to a small, gentle animal, a lamb, asking about its creator.
  2. Symbolic: The "Lamb" represents the state of "Innocence." It is also a direct Christian symbol for Jesus Christ, who is called the "Lamb of God" (Agnus Dei). The poem connects the child speaker, the lamb, and Jesus in a trinity of innocence.

Devices Deep-Dive: A Glossary

Apostrophe
A figure of speech where the speaker addresses an absent person, an abstract idea, or an object.
Example: "Little Lamb who made thee" and "Tyger Tyger, burning bright." In both poems, the speaker directly talks to the animal.
Rhetorical Question
A question asked not to receive an answer, but to make a point or create a dramatic effect.
Example: "Did he who made the Lamb make thee?" The poem is built on these questions, which express the speaker's awe and philosophical doubt.
Symbolism
The use of objects or ideas to represent something else.
Example: The Lamb symbolizes innocence, Christ, and gentle creation. The Tyger symbolizes experience, terrifying power, and the darker aspects of creation.
Allusion
An indirect reference to a person, place, event, or another literary work.
Example: The reference to Jesus as the "Lamb of God" in "The Lamb." The mention of seizing "fire" in "The Tyger" alludes to the Greek myth of Prometheus.
Metaphor
A comparison between two unlike things without using "like" or "as."
Example: "In what furnace was thy brain?" The tiger's creation is metaphorically compared to the work of a blacksmith in his forge.
Juxtaposition
Placing two contrasting elements side by side to highlight their differences.
Example: The entirety of Blake's project is a juxtaposition of Innocence ("The Lamb") and Experience ("The Tyger"). Within "The Tyger," the Lamb is explicitly mentioned to create a stark contrast.

Critical Analysis: The Two Contrary States of the Soul

William Blake's "The Lamb" and "The Tyger" are not merely two poems about animals; they are pillars of his visionary mythology, representing what he termed "The Two Contrary States of the Human Soul." Read together, they form a profound dialectic on the nature of God, creation, and human existence. "The Lamb," from Songs of Innocence, presents a world of gentle harmony, simple faith, and benevolent creation. "The Tyger," from Songs of Experience, shatters this worldview with its vision of a terrifying, violent, and awe-inspiring power.

The craftsmanship of each poem is perfectly tailored to its theme. "The Lamb" employs a simple, musical structure with a song-like trochaic meter and a question-and-answer format. The speaker, a child, asks a question and provides the answer with unwavering certainty. The creator is identified with Jesus, the "Lamb of God," who is "meek & mild." This world is safe, knowable, and governed by a loving, paternal God. The poem creates a unified trinity of the lamb, the child, and Christ, all sharing in a state of blessed innocence. There are no doubts, only comforting certainties.

In stark contrast, "The Tyger" is a barrage of unanswered questions. Its trochaic meter is not gentle but a hammering, industrial rhythm that evokes the blacksmith's forge. The central metaphor shifts from a pastoral shepherd to a divine, possibly demonic, blacksmith who "twists the sinews" and forges the tiger's brain in a furnace. The creative act is not gentle but violent, audacious, and terrifying. The poem‚Äôs speaker is not a confident child but an overwhelmed, awe-struck observer grappling with a creator who could fashion such "fearful symmetry." The crucial question, "Did he who made the Lamb make thee?" lies at the heart of the problem of theodicy‚Äîthe question of why a good God permits the manifestation of evil. The poem offers no answer, ending on a note of perpetual mystery by repeating the first stanza, but deepening the question by changing "Could" to "Dare."

Blake‚Äôs genius lies in his understanding that these two states are not simply "good" versus "evil." They are "contraries," not "negations." Innocence is a necessary state, but it is incomplete without the knowledge gained through Experience. Experience, with its pain, terror, and complexity (symbolized by the Tyger), is also a necessary part of the human journey and divine creation. One cannot fully understand the divine by only looking at the Lamb; one must also confront the Tyger. The creator of the Tyger is not necessarily evil, but a being of immense, untamable power, whose motives are beyond human comprehension. The poem suggests that creation, art, and even revolution require a terrifying, fiery energy that the gentle world of the Lamb cannot contain.

Today, these poems remain essential because they articulate fundamental questions that continue to challenge us. They force us to consider the duality within ourselves and the world: our capacity for both gentleness and ferocity, our desire for simple faith and our struggle with complex doubts. Blake does not provide an easy answer but instead offers a powerful vision: that a complete understanding of life requires embracing both the Lamb and the Tyger, the serene and the sublime, the comforting and the terrifying.
